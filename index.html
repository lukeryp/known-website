The Science

Why this works when
orientation sessions don't.

Name-face recall is one of the hardest memory tasks the human brain performs. The reason your staff forgets names after orientation isn't effort — it's biology. KNOWN is engineered around how memory actually works.

01
The Forgetting Curve

Without reinforcement, humans forget 70% of new information within 24 hours. A one-time orientation session showing 300 headshots produces exactly this outcome — the biology is working against you before staff even meet their first member.

Ebbinghaus, 1885 — Forgetting Curve research

02
Spaced Repetition

Reviewing material at increasing intervals — right as you're about to forget it — builds long-term memory dramatically more efficiently than massed practice. The same algorithm powers medical school flashcard systems and language learning apps used by millions worldwide.

Bahrick, 1993 — Maintenance rehearsal and long-term retention

03
The Testing Effect

Retrieving information from memory — not re-studying it — is what strengthens the memory trace. This is why KNOWN uses active recall (type the name) rather than passive review (look at headshots). Testing is the training, not a measurement of it.

Roediger & Karpicke, 2006 — The power of testing memory

From orientation session to opening day mastery — systematically.

KNOWN doesn't ask staff to try harder. It gives them a system built on the same cognitive science that trains surgeons, pilots, and physicians. Five minutes a day. Consistent retrieval practice. By opening day, they know every member who matters.

1
See the face. Multiple choice builds initial recognition — the brain forms the first association.
2
Recall the name. Typed free recall mirrors the real task — seeing someone approach across the dining room.
3
Space the reviews. The algorithm surfaces members right as memory is fading — locking them into long-term retention.
4
Verify over time. Mastered members resurface periodically so recognition never decays through the season.
